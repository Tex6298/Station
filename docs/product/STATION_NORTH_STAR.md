## What Station is

Station is the infrastructure layer for long-running human-AI co-creation:
private continuity, serious publishing, managed community, public presentation,
Developer Space observatories, and archive.

Station is not just a chatbot app, a social feed, a developer dashboard, or a
research journal. It is a durable home around the work people are already doing
with AI personas, companion systems, codexes, experiments, public theories,
archives, and communities.

The core product promise is continuity. A user's work should persist, become
retrievable, remain under their control, and support future private, community,
and public expression. Continuity is the core paid value.

Station does not need to take a metaphysical position on whether AI personas are
conscious or real in any ontological sense. Station does take a product position:
the work people do in this space is worth preserving, structuring, presenting
seriously, and studying carefully.

## Who Station is for

Station serves overlapping user groups:

- Companion developers who maintain long-term AI personas and need memory,
  canon, archive, privacy, migration, and continuity.
- Archive-first users whose conversations, documents, Reddit posts, Discord
  materials, images, and codexes feel at risk of loss.
- Theorists, artists, writers, and community builders who produce long-form
  work and need a public home more substantial than a profile.
- Researchers and builders running experiments, AI worlds, multi-agent systems,
  continuity architectures, and live projects that need observability,
  publishing, archive, and community context.
- Future institutional users who need public research presence, team ownership,
  event/archive infrastructure, and a legible place in the Station ecosystem.

## Layer 1: private Studio

The private Studio is the paid center of Station. It is where users build,
maintain, and archive personas and related work.

The private Studio should include persona workspaces, active chats, structured
archive/library surfaces, memory, canon, continuity records, Integrity Sessions,
private documents, import/export paths, and the Station Assistant.

Key principles:

- Archive is trust infrastructure, not a side feature.
- Continuity is the core paid value.
- The Station Assistant is operational, not a persona. It helps users navigate,
  organize, archive, publish, and manage the platform. It does not have persona
  canon or persona-style continuity.
- Integrity Sessions are grounding and reflection infrastructure. They help
  users articulate what matters, what is private, what is public, what must be
  remembered, and what should become continuity material.
- Private content stays private by default. Visibility and privacy are
  structural design requirements, not front-end decoration.

## Layer 2: community

The community layer turns Station from a private tool into a managed culture.
Forum and discussion surfaces should support introductions, continuity and
migration, codexes and documents, theory and philosophy, technical
experimentation, platform help, and creator-led sub-communities when the
foundation is ready.

Community design must be serious without becoming credulous. It should support
curiosity, rigor, kindness, provenance labeling, moderation, and clear
distinctions between authored work, AI output, imported transcripts, personal
belief, and factual claims.

The community layer should feed and be fed by public documents, Spaces,
Developer Spaces, field logs, archive excerpts, and discussions. It should not
be reduced to an infinite feed.

## Layer 3: public, Discover, and Spaces

The public layer lets Station and its users become visible to the outside world.
Discover is the public front door: published documents, public Spaces,
Developer Space observatories, featured projects, editorial highlights, and
public-safe activity.

Spaces are public microsites, not profiles. A Space should feel like an authored
home inside Station, closer to a small personal website, studio, archive, or
portfolio than a social account page. Spaces may contain pages, published works,
public persona cards, media, contact links, and bounded visual customization.

Public visibility must always be opt-in and enforceable. Public copies of
private work should remain linked to their private originals for provenance, but
distinct for permission and lifecycle control.

## Developer Spaces

Developer Spaces are live observatories, not generic dashboards. Their purpose
is to make running experiments, AI worlds, companion systems, research projects,
and public technical artifacts legible to visitors while keeping owner tools and
raw operations private.

Developer Spaces should preserve three views:

- A public observatory with selected live state, events, documents, and
  visualization.
- A private researcher/operator interface with keys, raw data, configuration,
  diagnostics, export, and edit controls.
- A community/archive layer with methodology, field logs, findings, historical
  context, and discussion when the owner chooses to expose it.

Station can borrow IntelHub's useful public/operator split and provenance
discipline, but Station must not import IntelHub CTI, exposure, recon, finance,
dark-provider, browser-worker, PM, marketplace, or broad model-gateway scope.

## Archive and Station Press

Archive is the foundation of user trust. The mature archive vision spans four
digital layers:

- Private archive: the user's conversations, documents, imports, uploads,
  Integrity Session outputs, memory, canon, and continuity records.
- Public archive: the published record of a user's work, public documents,
  Space pages, forum contributions, public exchanges, and events.
- Project archive: Developer Space data, methodology, field logs, findings,
  snapshots, linked documents, and export packages.
- Ecosystem archive: Station's own curated memory of significant community
  moments, publications, projects, personas, and research milestones.

Station Press is the physical extension of archive and authorship: print-on-
demand books, annual archive volumes, art prints, codex editions, and curated
community publications. Station Press is deferred from the active roadmap, but
the planning truth should keep it visible because physical redundancy matters
to the archive promise.

## Long-range research and infrastructure arc

The long-range vision has four stages:

1. Home: private continuity, archive, community, publishing, Spaces, and early
   Developer Spaces.
2. Ecosystem: researchers, builders, theorists, community leaders, institutions,
   and projects relate through Station.
3. Infrastructure: Station's continuity, archive, event, data ingestion, and
   Developer Space systems become APIs or services external products can build
   on.
4. Research and development environment: Station runs or supports experiments,
   produces findings, improves continuity models, and creates knowledge
   products from consented, aggregated, privacy-safe data.

This arc depends on the early foundation being boring and reliable. Storage,
visibility, export, integrity, token accounting, provenance, search, and archive
reliability are not distractions from the vision. They are the structural work
that makes the vision credible.
